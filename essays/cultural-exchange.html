On Cultural Exchange and Belonging

February 8, 2026

The first morning in Kyoto, I woke to the sound of temple bells. It was a sound I had encountered in films and read about in literature, but experiencing it physically created an unexpected rupture in my understanding of cultural distance.

What I discovered during the Asia Kakehashi Project was not simply exposure to a different geography, but a new lens through which to examine my own culture. The Japanese concept of "ma" — the intentional use of negative space — made me reconsider the Indian aesthetic principle of horror vacui, our cultural tendency to fill every surface with pattern and ornament.

Neither approach is superior. Both are responses to different philosophical priorities. But understanding this required physical displacement, the kind that only comes from standing in a minimalist tea room after growing up surrounded by maximalist temple architecture.

The Paradox of Distance

Cultural exchange programs are often marketed as opportunities to "understand others." What they rarely advertise is that the primary understanding you gain is about yourself.

"The stranger sees what the native cannot see precisely because what the native sees is structured by long familiarity."

This is not to romanticize displacement. There is real difficulty in being perpetually out of context. But there is also a clarity that comes from occupying that liminal space.
