Kakehashi (架け橋) means bridge. The program builds one between you and a country that will not make sense at first — and then, slowly, will start to feel like somewhere you actually live. This guide covers everything from the application to the shape of the year itself, written by someone who has been on both sides.

01

What is the Asia Kakehashi Project?

概要

The Asia Kakehashi Project is a fully-funded Japanese government scholarship that sends high school students from across Asia to Japan for a full academic year. Run through the Japan Ministry of Foreign Affairs and JICE, it places you inside a regular Japanese public high school — not a special program, not an international track. Your classmates are Japanese. Your teachers speak Japanese. You adapt, or you figure out how to adapt, which is the whole point.

Program · 01
Fully Funded
Flights, host family, meals, school placement, health insurance, monthly stipend. The Japanese government covers everything. Your cost is zero.
Program · 02
Real School Placement
A regular Japanese public high school as a real enrolled student. Not a visitor. Your classmates are Japanese. The social dynamics, the clubs, the classroom rules — you are inside all of it.
Program · 03
Host Family Living
You live with a Japanese family for the year. Not a dormitory, not a guesthouse. A real home. This is where most of the actual learning happens.
What it is not

Kakehashi is not a language program, not a tourism experience, not a gap year. You are not there to accumulate stories. You are there to live inside a society and come to understand it from within. The discomfort is the point.

02

Who Can Apply

応募資格

The formal eligibility criteria are not difficult to meet. The actual competition is a separate matter — 21 slots from all of India means the field thins quickly at each stage.

  • Currently enrolled in high school in India — 9th or 10th grade is optimal
  • Age typically 15–18 at the time of departure for Japan
  • Able to communicate in English — no Japanese required at application stage
  • Available for the full program duration — a year, not a semester
  • No prior participation in Japanese government exchange programs (verify against current year guidelines)
The actual competition

The formal bar is low. The real filter is the essay and interview. Strong grades help but they are not the primary selector. A weak essay cannot be saved by excellent marks. A strong essay can survive average marks.

05

The Essay — How to Actually Win It

小論文の書き方

The essay is where most applications are decided. Committees read hundreds of submissions from students with similar grades and credentials. The essay is the only place where you are completely irreplaceable.

What I Did · 2021

My essay was specific. Not "I love Japanese culture" but an actual observation I had made — about how a society that prizes collective silence produces individuals who are quietly extraordinary. Not "I want to visit Japan" but a concrete gap I'd noticed between how Japan is perceived and how it actually functions.

I connected it to something I would bring back and actually do. I had already published a book. I framed the exchange as research — not vaguely, but concretely. The committee funds people who will use the experience, not just have it.

"Show them you've thought about Japan specifically. Not just about wanting to go somewhere interesting."

I also did not try to sound older than I was. I was 16. I wrote like someone 16 and genuinely curious. They read hundreds of essays from students performing diplomacy. Write like a person who has actually thought about this.

What works

  • Specificity over generality. "I'm interested in how Japanese concepts of ma (間) shape design thinking" beats "I love Japanese culture" immediately. Show you've engaged with something real.
  • A real reason, not a performed one. If it's manga, own that and connect it meaningfully — how visual storytelling in manga uses silence differently than Western comics. Authenticity is always legible.
  • What you will do with it. Be specific about what you bring back. The program funds cultural diplomacy, not tourism.
  • Your voice, not a template. A distinct voice is noticed in the first paragraph. Every year they read thousands of identical essays.

What doesn't work

  • Lists of achievements with no connection to Japan specifically
  • "I have always dreamed of visiting Japan" without any evidence of actual engagement
  • Generic statements about cultural bridges and global citizenship
  • Trying to sound more formal or mature than you are — it reads as hollow
06

The Interview Round

面接について

If you receive an interview invitation, your essay worked. The committee now wants to see if you are the person who wrote it. Read your essay the morning of the interview — know every claim you made in it.

Question they will askWhat they are actually checking
Why Japan specifically, not another country?
Whether your interest is real and specific, not just wanderlust. Have a concrete answer rooted in something you've actually engaged with.
How will you handle the language barrier?
Adaptability. Mention you've started learning basics. Do not claim fluency.
What will you miss most about home?
Emotional honesty. "Nothing" is not believable. Say something real and explain why you'd still go.
What will you bring back to India from this?
Intentionality. Have a specific answer about what you'll do with the experience.
How do you handle discomfort or conflict?
Resilience. The year will be hard. They need to believe you won't become a problem in month two.
Tell us about something you've made or built.
Agency. They prefer students who create things over students who receive experiences.
My interview · what I remember

My hands were not steady. My voice was. I had practiced answers out loud, not just in my head — that made the difference. They asked me to go deeper on a claim I'd made in my essay. I could, because I'd actually thought about it. The essay was the beginning of your thinking, not the end of it.

07

What Actually Happens During the Year

プログラム内容
Month 1–2 · The Fog

You understand almost nothing

Not the language, the social rules, the school system, or the food. This is normal. Your only job is observation — watch everything, ask less, notice more. The patterns start emerging around week six.

Month 3–5 · The System

You learn how things work

The train schedule. The school rhythms. The unspoken classroom rules. You begin having real conversations. You find your rhythm. The year starts becoming genuinely enjoyable rather than just exhausting.

Month 6–9 · The Integration

Japan stops feeling foreign

You have a group, or at least a few people who know you. A club. Japan feels like somewhere you actually live. This is the best part of the year and it passes faster than you expect.

Month 10–12 · The Ending

You start to know you are leaving

Formal goodbye events, gifts, speeches. Your host family will say things that don't translate cleanly but that you'll understand completely. The last bus ride to school feels different from the first.

The thing nobody says

The hardest part is not the language barrier or the homesickness, though both are real. It is the middle period — when you understand enough to see clearly how far outside you still are. Push through. Month seven is when it breaks open.
