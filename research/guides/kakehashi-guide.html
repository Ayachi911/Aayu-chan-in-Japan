Kakehashi (架け橋) means bridge. The program builds one between you and a country that will leave you awestruck at first and then, slowly, will start to feel like somewhere you actually live. This guide covers everything from the application to the shape of the year itself, written by someone who has been on both sides.

01

What is the Asia Kakehashi Project+?

概要

The Asia Kakehashi Project (AKP) is a fully-funded Japanese government scholarship that sends high school students to Japan for a real cultural exchange. Run in partnership with MEXT and AFS, it places you inside a regular Japanese public high school as a real enrolled student — not a visitor, not a special international track. Since 2023, the program rebranded as AKP Plus, expanding from Asia-only to 27 countries including G7 nations, while the cohort shrank from ~250 to ~100 students globally and duration reduced from a full year to four months. This guide is written from my 2021 experience — a full ten-month year — so where the current program differs, I note it clearly.

Program · 01
Fully Funded
Round-trip flights, host family, dormitory, meals, school placement, transportation, health insurance, monthly stipend. The Japanese government covers everything. Your cost is zero.
Program · 02
Real School Placement
A regular Japanese public high school as a real enrolled student. Not a visitor. Your classmates are Japanese. The social dynamics, the clubs, the classroom rules — you are inside all of it.
Program · 03
Host Family Living
You live with a Japanese family for the year. If none allocated, a dormitory. This is where most of the actual learning happens. You will commute to school from your host family's neighbourhood or the dorm— see the daily commute guide for what that looks like in practice.
What it is not

Kakehashi is not a language program, not a tourism experience, not a gap year. You are not there to accumulate stories. You are there to live inside a society and come to understand it from within. The discomfort is the point.

¥0 cost ≠ ¥0 spending

The program covers flights, school placement, host family, meals, insurance, and a monthly stipend. Your out-of-pocket cost to participate is genuinely zero. But you will spend money — on manga and otaku goods, rental kimonos, cafes, day trips (I went to Tokyo Disneyland — full trip post here), and the kind of small daily spending that accumulates fast in Japan. Budget for it. The stipend helps but won't cover everything if you're buying things.

02

Who Can Apply

応募資格

The formal eligibility criteria are not difficult to meet. The actual competition is a separate matter — 21 slots from all of India in 2021 means the field thins quickly at each stage. AKP Plus runs smaller: approximately 100 students globally across 27 countries.

  • Currently enrolled in high school in India — 9th to 12th grade; 9th or 10th is optimal for the full-year legacy program, check current guidelines
  • Minimum 60% in current grade — this is the first Google Form filter. Below this threshold, applications are screened out before review
  • Age typically 15–18 at time of departure
  • Able to communicate in English; any Japanese is a BIG bonus
  • Available for the full program duration — currently four months (AKP Plus); was a full academic year in 2021
  • No prior participation in Japanese government exchange programs (verify against current year guidelines)
The actual competition

The formal bar is low. The real filter is the essay and interview. Strong grades help but they are not the primary selector. A weak essay cannot be saved by excellent marks. An impressive essay can survive average marks. All rounders and students who demonstrate strong character are preferred.

03

Application Timeline

出願スケジュール

The structure below reflects how selection ran for my 2021 cohort. Specific dates shift year to year — for 2026 applications from India, the deadline has already passed. Watch AFS India's website and set a reminder for next year's cycle the moment you finish reading this.

How I found out about this program

I was in tenth class, scrolling Google late at night — "how to study in Japanese high school" — because I genuinely wanted that slice-of-life anime school experience. I found a Quora answer from a girl who'd done it. That sent me to AFS India. I missed the deadline that year by a month. I kept an eye open, contacted the AFS team for updates, and applied the moment forms dropped the next cycle. If you are reading this because you're doing a similar midnight search: you are in the right place.

StageRoundWhat this means for you
Step 1
⚑ Google Form Screen
A short initial form asking basic eligibility questions — including whether you scored above 60% in your current grade. This is the first filter. Below 60%, you are screened out. Keep your academics up.
Step 2
Pre-Application Form
Long. Very long. Covers academic record, co-curricular achievements, two essays. The topics may vary year to year — mine included questions similar to the AFS forms. Start early. This is where most people underestimate the time required.
Step 3
⚑ Interview (8–14 min)
Shortlisted candidates are called for an interview lasting between 8 and 14 minutes. See Section 06 for full prep. This round is decisive — prepare your essay claims to defend in depth.
Step 4
Home Call
If you receive a home call from AFS India, this almost certainly means selection. Treat it as confirmation while waiting for the formal letter.
+
Waitlist
AFS also releases a waitlist. Don't dismiss it — my 2021 batch had three withdrawals, which moved the top waitlisted students in. If you're on the list, stay ready and keep your documents current.
Final
⚑ Pre-Departure
2021 was fully online due to COVID — Zoom orientation sessions. Current cycles run in-person. Orientation in Japan follows, then placement with host family and school.
2026 Status — India

Applications for India's 2026 cycle are closed. The deadline and selection timeline varies by country — for the current program year across all 27 participating nations, check AFS in your country directly. The AKP Plus now runs both Asia and G7 tracks.

05

The Essay — How to Actually Win It

小論文の書き方

The essay is where most applications are decided. Committees read hundreds of submissions from students with similar grades and credentials. The essay is the only place where you are completely irreplaceable.

Important — essay topics change every year

AFS India changes the essay prompts each cycle. Do not write to any fixed topic from a previous year's guide, including this one. Contact your AFS volunteer to get the current year's prompts before you write a single word. The advice below is about approach and quality, not topic.

Honest Account · 2021

My first draft was not impressive. I wrote the kind of essay most students write— a list of achievements, a vague love of Japanese culture, a statement about wanting to become a global citizen. It wasn't bad but very forgettable.

A senior who had gone to Japan the year before read it and gave me honest direction. He told me it felt corporate and soulless. Dang bro didn't have to be that direct

"It read like a resume, not a person." — the feedback that changed mine.

If you have access to a previous scholar — a senpai who went through the same process — ask them to read your draft. Not to copy their approach, but to get that same outside-eye honesty. Their experience is the most useful resource you have.

What works

  • A real reason, not a performed one. If it's anime or manga, you can own that — but connect it to something you've actually engaged with beyond watching. What did it make you curious about? What question do you actually have about Japan?
  • Specificity over generality. "I want to become a global citizen" is in every essay. An observation you've made, something you've noticed, something you've already made — that's yours alone.
  • Lists of achievements even with no connection to Japan or the program specifically. All-rounders have a very sharp edge.
  • What you will do with it. The program funds cultural diplomacy, not tourism. Be specific about what you bring back.
  • Your actual voice. Don't try to sound older or more formal than you are. They can tell. Write like a person who has genuinely thought about this, not like someone performing seriousness.

What doesn't work

  • "I have always dreamed of visiting Japan" with no evidence of actual engagement
  • Skimping out on even minor achievements thinking, "Nah, it isn't impressive.". You never know what the panel might actually like. It all comes back to how you present your achievements.
  • Generic statements about cultural bridges and global citizenship
  • Copying structure from a previous year's selected application — committees could notice patterns
06

The Interview Round

面接について

If you receive an interview invitation, your essay worked. The committee now wants to see if you are the person who wrote it. Read your essay the morning of the interview — know every claim you made in it.

Question they will askWhat they are actually checking
Why Japan specifically, not another country?
Whether your interest is real and specific, not just wanderlust. Have a concrete answer rooted in something you've actually engaged with.
How will you handle the language barrier?
Adaptability. Mention you've started learning basics. Do not claim fluency.
What will you miss most about home?
Emotional honesty. "Nothing" is not believable. Say something real and explain why you'd still go.
What will you bring back to India from this?
Intentionality. Have a specific answer about what you'll do with the experience.
How do you handle discomfort or conflict?
Resilience. The year will be hard. They need to believe you won't become a problem in month two.
Tell us about something you've made or built.
Agency. They prefer students who create things over students who receive experiences.
My interview · what I remember

My hands were not steady. My voice was. I had practiced answers out loud, not just in my head — that made the difference. They asked me to go deeper on a claim I'd made in my essay. I could, because I'd actually thought about it. The essay was the beginning of your thinking, not the end of it.

What Actually Happens During the Year

プログラム内容
Note — 2021 vs current program

This section describes my full ten-month year (2021 program). The current AKP Plus runs four months. The arc below still maps accurately to the emotional shape of exchange — the phases just compress. The fog is still real in month one. The integration still happens, faster.

Month 1–2 · The Fog

You understand almost nothing

Not the language, the social rules, the school system, or the food. This is normal. Your only job is observation — watch everything, ask less, notice more. You can read what a full school day actually looks like here — the classes, the lunch, the after-school clubs. The patterns start emerging around week six.

Month 3–5 · The System

You learn how things work

The train schedule. The school rhythms. The unspoken classroom rules. You begin having real conversations. You find your rhythm. The year starts becoming genuinely enjoyable rather than just exhausting.

Month 6–9 · The Integration

Japan stops feeling foreign

You have a group, or at least a few people who know you. A club. Japan feels like somewhere you actually live. This is the best part of the year and it passes faster than you expect.

Month 10–12 · The Ending

You start to know you are leaving

Formal goodbye events, gifts, speeches. Your host family will say things that don't translate cleanly but that you'll understand completely. The last bus ride to school feels different from the first.

The thing nobody says

The hardest part is not the language barrier or the homesickness, though both are real. It is the middle period — when you understand enough to see clearly how far outside you still are. Push through. Month seven is when it breaks open.
