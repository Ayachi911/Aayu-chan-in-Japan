How to Actually Get to a Japanese High School Every Day 神奈台から平沼高校まで — the 49-minute commute

Bus from Kounandai. Train to Yokohama. Walk fifteen minutes uphill. Repeat 200 times. And one morning the gates closed right in front of my outstretched hand.

Every exchange student worries about the commute before they arrive. What if I take the wrong train? What if I miss the bus? What if I can't read the signs? I am here to tell you: you will figure it out. And you will probably also, at least once, stand outside closing bus doors with your hand stretched out going noooooo. That's fine too. This is that story, and also a practical guide.

01

The Morning Routine — 7:00 → 7:30

朝のルーティン

I woke up at 7:00. This sounds early but it is not, by Japanese high school standards — many of my classmates were already on trains by then. My host family's house was in Kounandai (神奈台), a residential neighbourhood that sits slightly elevated above the surrounding streets — not quite a hill, but definitely the kind of neighbourhood where you notice the incline after a heavy lunch.

By 7:30 I was out the door in my seifuku — uniform on, bento box packed by okāsan and waiting on the kitchen counter (制服 — school uniform). This was non-negotiable. The bus to the station left at a specific time, and the Japanese concept of "I'll catch the next one" does not work the same way it does at home. The next bus wasn't in ten minutes. It was in forty.

Tip · ヒント

Lay out your uniform the night before. This sounds obvious. Do it anyway. You will be too sleepy at 7:00 to make decisions about where your seifuku necktie went.

02

The Uphill Walk + Bus Stop

バス停まで · 5分

Five minutes of walking, mostly uphill, to the bus stop. In winter this was cold and quiet and I could see my breath. In the rain — which happened more than you'd think — I had an umbrella and the Japanese knack for keeping it slightly tilted against the wind that I learned by watching everyone else.

The bus stop sign read 学校前 (gakkō mae — "in front of school"). The yellow sign on the pole was a polite notice explaining why the bench was positioned toward the road: the footpath was too narrow to put it the usual way. Even bus stop furniture in Japan comes with a written apology.

03

Bus 111 — 11 Minutes to the Station

バスで駅まで · 11分

The bus was yellow-gold and navy — the Yokohama municipal bus colours. It came at the time the schedule said it would come. Every time. If you have only ever used buses in a country where the schedule is a suggestion, the first week of this will feel surreal.

Yokohama Bus Terminal · The buses come in the correct colour for a city that takes transit seriously

I paid with my IC card — a prepaid card that works on buses, trains, and convenience stores across Japan. You tap when you get on. You tap when you get off. The fare calculates automatically. It is the smoothest payment experience I have ever had, and going back to cash after returning to India felt like going back to dial-up internet.

Essential · IC Card

Get a Suica or PASMO card the day you arrive. (See the full IC card setup guide if you need the step-by-step.) Load ¥3,000 to start. It works on everything. Yokohama buses accept IC cards and the machine beeps differently if your balance is too low — learn that beep.

About the Driver

I think he recognised me. I was the only Indian kid on this route, dressed in a Japanese high school uniform, every weekday morning without fail. Japanese bus drivers are extremely professional and show very little expression, but there is something in the way a person's eyes acknowledge you after the fortieth time you've boarded their bus that communicates: I know you. You belong on this bus.

I never knew his name. I never spoke to him beyond sumimasen and the occasional confused fumble with my IC card. But I think about him sometimes. He was, in the most literal sense, part of how I got to school every day.

04

The Train — 23 Minutes

電車で · 23分

At the station, I transferred to the densha (電車). Twenty-three minutes to Yokohama, roughly. I usually stood. Everyone stands in the morning — unless you are elderly, pregnant, or carrying something large, you give up your seat, and you do this without being asked because the social norm is that clear.

Platform 8 · The tactile yellow strip is everywhere. Platform markings show exactly where each car door will open.

The platform had coloured markers on the ground showing exactly where each car's doors would open. You stood in line at the marker. When the train arrived, the doors opened exactly there. You boarded. It was so efficient it felt almost choreographed — which, in a sense, it was.

Through the train window approaching Yokohama. That building with the LaVista sign became a landmark I used to know I was almost there.

What I Did on the Train

Looked out the window, mostly. Watched Yokohama's residential streets become denser, then commercial, then urban. Occasionally reviewed vocab. Never talked loudly. Trains in Japan are quiet — not silent, but consciously quiet. People wear earphones. Conversations are low. Phones are on silent. I adjusted to this within a week and found it surprisingly peaceful.

the story · 物語
06

The Morning I Almost Missed Everything

めずらしい朝
What Actually Happened · 実話

It was a Saturday or a Sunday — I can't remember which now, but I remember that the bus schedule was different. Fewer buses. The kind of timetable adjustment that exists for weekends and holidays and that I had never needed to think about because weekdays were always the same.

I arrived at the bus stop. I saw the bus. The bus was at the stop. I walked toward it — and then I watched the doors close. Right in front of me. I stretched out my hand, dramatically, in the universal gesture of noooooo, fully aware that this was a movie cliché and doing it anyway.

The bus halted. The doors opened.

I don't know why. I don't know if the driver saw me in his mirror. I don't know if he made an exception for the Indian exchange student he'd been driving to school for months. I don't know if this was mezurashii (珍しい — unusual, rare) or just a moment of human discretion in a system that is otherwise a machine.

I climbed on. I bowed my head and whispered sumimasen. The driver hit the accelerator immediately. I almost lost my balance. He didn't look at me. He was already back in the rhythm of the route, schedule partially recovered, moving on.

Japanese punctuality is not cruelty. It is respect — for every other passenger who budgeted exactly this amount of time to get to exactly where they needed to be. The driver was late by approximately thirty seconds because of me. I thought about that the whole ride.

07

Practical Things You Actually Need to Know

実用的なヒント

IC Cards

Get one on arrival. Suica (JR) and PASMO (private lines) both work across most of Japan interchangeably. Load enough balance to not have the low-beep embarrassment. You can top up at any convenience store or station machine.

Reading the Timetable

Japanese bus and train timetables list departure times, not arrival windows. The bus leaves at 07:35. This means it leaves at 07:35. Not 07:36. Note which stops are listed and which direction you need before you arrive at the stop.

Weekend Schedules

Buses run differently on Saturdays, Sundays, and public holidays. The difference can be significant — sometimes half as frequent. If you have somewhere to be on a weekend morning, check the weekend timetable the night before.

Yokohama Station

Large enough that navigating it the first time will take extra minutes. Give yourself a buffer on the first day. The walk to Hiranuma is not complicated once you know the direction, but Yokohama station exits are not all the same — find the right exit first.

Honest Warning · 正直なアドバイス

You will miss the bus at least once. It is fine. Wait for the next one. Message your host family if you'll be late. The world does not end. The bus driver who saved me taught me that the system has more flexibility than it appears — but don't test it twice.

· · ·

The commute was never just transportation. It was an hour of being in Japan — the wet roads, the quiet trains, the bus that came when it said it would. By month three I stopped noticing it consciously. By month six I could have done it in my sleep. By the time I left, I missed it.

That uphill walk from the bus stop to school, in my seifuku, with my bag on one shoulder, in whatever weather was happening that morning — that was mine. It belonged to that year in a way very few things do.
