What to Pack for a Year in Japan as a High School Exchange Student 🧳

By Ayachi Samyal, Asia Kakehashi Scholar 2021 · Yokohama, Japan · Last updated February 2026 · ⏱ 15 min read

I packed for Japan at 16 with no idea what I was doing. I brought things I never touched and left behind things I desperately needed by month two. This is the list I wish I'd had — tested over a full year in Yokohama.

💡 Most important rule: You will have access to shops in Japan. Bring less than you think you need. Japanese stores are incredible and you'll want room in your bag for the trip home.

1. The Non-Negotiables

These are things where if you forget them, no Japanese shop can fix it.

Documents (keep originals + 3 copies of each)

  • Passport (valid for the full program duration + 6 months)
  • Student visa / program documents from the embassy
  • School enrollment letter
  • Program acceptance letter
  • Health insurance card — your Indian card won't cover Japan
  • Emergency contact list (printed, not just on phone)
  • Host family details (address, phone, printed separately)

2. Clothes: What Japan Actually Requires

⚠️ Shoe warning: If you're above a women's size 7 (EU 38) or men's size 9 (EU 43), Japanese shoe sizes will be very limited. Bring multiple pairs of the shoes you'll wear most.

You'll wear school uniform most of the time

Your school will provide or tell you how to get your uniform. You won't need as many "going out" clothes as you think. Japanese high school is very uniform-heavy — I wore mine 5 days a week.

✅ Bring

  • 5–6 casual outfits (weekends/breaks)
  • 1 formal outfit (ceremonies, events)
  • Comfortable walking shoes × 2 pairs
  • Indoor slippers (required in schools)
  • Winter coat (Yokohama gets cold)
  • Thermal underlayers × 3
  • Socks × 10 pairs minimum
  • Swimwear (school PE may include pool)

❌ Leave behind

  • Fancy dresses (almost no occasions)
  • Heavy boots (storage issues in Japanese homes)
  • More than 2 bags/purses
  • Too many graphic tees (subtle styles preferred in schools)
  • Full-length winter coat if going in spring
  • Pajamas (buy cute ones in Japan, much better)

3. Tech Setup

Power Adapters

Japan uses Type A plugs (same as US/Canada) at 100V. Indian plugs are Type C/D at 230V. You need a voltage converter for anything that isn't dual-voltage (check your device label for "100–240V" — if it says that, you only need a plug adapter).

4. School Supplies

Good news: Japanese stationery is incredible and you'll want to buy it there. Don't bring lots of pens and notebooks — you'll end up replacing them anyway with Japanese ones that are genuinely better.

Bring from India

  • Your preferred calculator (if you have one)
  • Any study materials in your native language you'll need
  • A good dictionary app downloaded offline (Japanese ↔ English)

Buy in Japan

  • Notebooks — Kokuyo Campus notebooks are legendary
  • Pens — Pilot, Uni, Zebra are all far superior to most Indian options
  • Sticky notes, highlighters, correction tape
  • Pencil case — 100 yen stores have great ones

5. Comfort Items That Saved Me

These are the "soft" items that sound unnecessary but genuinely help with homesickness and settling in.

  • Photos printed on paper — of your family, friends, home. Put them up in your room at the host family's house. It sounds small but it matters a lot.
  • A comfort food you love from India — not a full bag, just a few packs of something. Instant noodles, biscuits, whatever your version is. For the first two weeks of homesickness this is genuinely helpful.
  • Your own pillow — controversial because of bag space, but Japanese pillows tend to be harder than what most Indians are used to. A travel pillow at minimum.
  • A journal — you will want to write things down. The year goes fast and your memory will not keep up.

6. What to Buy in Japan Instead

Save bag space and money by buying these there. Japan is cheaper for these categories than India.

  • Skincare and face masks (Japanese drugstores are unbeatable)
  • Umbrellas (cheap, everywhere, buy one there)
  • Stationery as mentioned above
  • Cute room decorations (Daiso, Seria 100¥ stores)
  • Rain gear and seasonal clothes you underestimated
  • Snacks for daily life — Japanese convenience stores (konbini) are legendary

7. What to Leave Behind

Things that seem necessary but you genuinely won't use or can easily replace:

  • More than 2 pairs of jeans — you'll be in uniform most of the time
  • Fancy shoes — Japanese streets require practical footwear
  • Heavy textbooks — most academic resources can be digital
  • Full-size toiletries — Japanese drugstores have everything, often better quality
  • Your entire makeup collection — a small kit is fine
  • Gaming gear, large electronics — too heavy, too much hassle at customs
⚠️ Customs note: Japan has strict customs rules around certain medications and supplements. If you take prescription medication, carry a doctor's letter in both English and Japanese if possible, and check Japan's customs rules specifically for your medication before packing.
