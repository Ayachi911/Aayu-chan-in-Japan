What to Pack for a Year in Japan 一年分の荷造り — tested list

I packed for Japan at 16 with no idea what I was doing. I brought things I never touched and left behind things I desperately needed by month two. This is the list I wish I'd had.

The most important rule: you will have access to shops when you get there. Extraordinary shops. Daiso, Tokyu Hands, every convenience store on earth. The instinct to over-pack "just in case" costs bag space and carry-on fees. Bring less than you think. Leave room for the trip home — you will need it.

Disclosure: Some links below are affiliate links. I only recommend things I actually used or wish I'd had. You pay nothing extra.

01
Non-Negotiables絶対必要なもの

These are items where if you forget them, no Japanese shop can fix it. Get these sorted before you think about anything else.

Documents — carry originals + three printed copies of each

  • Passport — valid for the full program duration plus at least 6 months beyond
  • Student visa and all program acceptance documents from the embassy
  • School enrollment letter from your Indian school
  • Health insurance documentation — your Indian card does not cover Japan
  • Emergency contact list printed on paper, not just saved on your phone
  • Host family address and phone number printed separately

Money setup

  • ¥50,000–¥80,000 cash for your first two weeks before a local bank account is set up
  • International debit card — tell your bank you are travelling before you leave
  • Wise card — real exchange rates, low fees, works seamlessly in Japan

Travel Insurance — non-negotiable

Japan has excellent healthcare but it is expensive if you are uninsured. Check exactly what your program covers. If there are gaps, get supplementary coverage before you leave. This is not optional.

02
Clothes服装 · what Japan actually requires
Shoe warning

If you are above a women's size 7 (EU 38) or men's size 9 (EU 43), Japanese shoe sizes will be very limited. Bring multiple pairs of the shoes you will wear most. This is the one category where over-packing is justified.

Your school will provide or tell you how to get your uniform. You will wear it five days a week. You need far fewer "going out" clothes than you think.

03
Tech Setup電子機器

Power adapters

Japan uses Type A plugs (same as US/Canada) at 100V. India uses Type C/D at 230V. You need a voltage converter for anything that is not dual-voltage — check your device label for "100–240V." If it says that, you only need a plug adapter, not a full converter.

SIM card and data

Your Indian SIM will not work well in Japan on a regular plan. For your first few weeks while you set up a local plan, an eSIM is the easiest option. Once you have a Japanese address, IIJmio or Rakuten Mobile are the most cost-effective long-term options.

  • Portable charger — 10,000mAh minimum, you will be out all day
  • Earphones — commutes are long (49 minutes in my case, every day)
  • Download Google Maps offline for your city before departure
  • Laptop if you plan to write or study seriously
04
School Supplies学用品

Japanese stationery is extraordinary and you will want to buy it there. Do not pack lots of pens and notebooks — you will replace them anyway with Japanese ones that are genuinely better.

Bring from India

  • Your preferred calculator if you have one
  • Any study materials you need in English or your native language
  • A dictionary app downloaded offline — Japanese to English, English to Japanese

Buy in Japan instead

  • Notebooks — Kokuyo Campus notebooks are legendary. You will buy six.
  • Pens — Pilot, Uni, Zebra are all far superior to most options back home
  • Sticky notes, highlighters, correction tape, pencil case from 100 yen stores
100 yen stores — Daiso, Seria

Everything you could possibly need for your school desk, your room, your bag, and your life in general — at 110 yen per item. Budget ¥3,000–5,000 for a Daiso run in your first week and just enjoy it.

PART TWO · WHAT SAVES YOU
05
Comfort Items安心グッズ · the ones that saved me

These are the "soft" items that sound unnecessary but genuinely help with the first month, which is the hardest month.

  • Photos printed on paper. Of your family, friends, home. Put them up in your room. It sounds small. It is not small. Do this.
  • One comfort food from India. Not a full bag. Just a few packs of something — instant noodles, biscuits, whatever your version is. For the first two weeks of homesickness this is genuinely helpful.
  • A journal. You will want to write things down. The year goes fast. Your memory will not keep up.
  • A small thing from home that means something. Not for showing anyone. Just for knowing it is there.
06
Buy in Japan現地調達リスト

These categories are cheaper and better in Japan than anything you could pack. Save the bag space.

  • Skincare and face masks — Japanese drugstores (Matsumoto Kiyoshi, Sundrug) are genuinely extraordinary
  • Umbrellas — cheap, everywhere, buy one when it first rains and keep it in your bag
  • All stationery as described above
  • Room decorations and organisers from 100 yen stores
  • Any seasonal clothing you underestimated — Japan has distinct seasons and you will need to adapt
07
Customs & Meds税関と薬
Important — medication rules

Japan has strict customs rules around certain medications and supplements. If you take prescription medication, carry a doctor's letter in both English and Japanese if possible, and check Japan's customs website specifically for your medication before you pack. Some common medications in India are controlled substances in Japan.

General medications — basic pain relief, antacids, cold medicine — are available at Japanese drugstores. However, formulations differ from Indian versions and reading the packaging in Japanese can be difficult at first. Bring a two-month supply of anything you rely on regularly and restock carefully once you have someone who can help you read labels.
