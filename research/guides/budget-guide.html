1. What the Program Actually Covers

The Kakehashi Project is a Japanese government scholarship. Here's what's genuinely included:

  • Round-trip flights — India to Japan and back
  • Host family accommodation — you live with a Japanese family, no housing cost
  • School enrollment — your Japanese high school placement is covered
  • Basic meal support — host family typically provides breakfast and dinner
  • Program activities — field trips, cultural events, school events are covered
  • Monthly stipend — a small amount for incidentals (varies by program year, typically ¥10,000–¥15,000/month)
💡 Key point: The stipend sounds like spending money but it's really meant to cover lunch and transport. It's not enough to cover everything you'll actually spend. Factor in additional monthly support from home.

2. What It Doesn't Cover

These are the costs your family needs to actually budget for:

  • Any medication you need regularly
  • Personal shopping (clothes, toiletries, stationery)
  • Eating out with friends and weekend activities
  • Travel within Japan on weekends/breaks
  • Gifts for your host family (expected culturally — bring something from India and budget for occasional gifts during the year)
  • Phone plan / data
  • Supplementary travel insurance (see section 4)
  • Emergency costs

4. Travel Insurance — The One You Can't Skip

Japan has world-class healthcare. It also charges full-price for it if you're not covered. A single hospital visit without insurance can cost ₹15,000–₹50,000. A serious emergency can run into lakhs.

Your Kakehashi program provides some baseline coverage. Read the documents carefully — check what's excluded. Common gaps include:

  • Dental (not covered by most program policies)
  • Pre-existing conditions
  • Mental health support
  • Emergency evacuation back to India

5. How to Send Money to Japan from India

Your parents will need to top up your account periodically. Here's the actual landscape:

Wise — Best option for most families

Wise transfers at mid-market exchange rates with transparent fees. A ₹15,000 transfer will arrive as close to the real rate as you'll get. Transfer time is usually 1–3 business days to a Japanese bank account.

💸 Wise — Set This Up Before You Leave India

Both you and a parent should create Wise accounts before departure. Test a small transfer to make sure everything works. Once you have a Japanese bank account (you'll get one through the program), set it up as a recipient. This is far cheaper than bank-to-bank SWIFT transfers.

Japanese bank account setup

You'll likely get help setting up a Japanese bank account (Japan Post Bank or similar) through your program. This takes a few weeks — your host family helps. Until then, bring enough cash for 3–4 weeks and use your Wise debit card at ATMs.

💡 Japan is still very cash-heavy. Not everywhere accepts cards — especially smaller restaurants, shrines, some school events. Always carry ¥3,000–¥5,000 cash on you.

7. Money-Saving Tips That Actually Work

  • IC card (Suica/Pasmo) — load it up and use it for all train/bus travel. Cheaper than buying individual tickets, works everywhere.
  • Konbini lunch — Japanese convenience stores (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) sell genuinely good meals for ¥400–¥600. Way cheaper than school canteens or restaurants for daily lunch.
  • 100 yen stores — Daiso and Seria sell quality everyday items for ¥100. Cleaning supplies, stationery, kitchen items, storage. You'll end up there weekly.
  • Cash budget system — withdraw a fixed amount each week and when it's gone, it's gone. Works better than tracking on your phone for most students.
  • Student discounts — carry your student ID everywhere. Many museums, trains, and attractions offer significant student discounts.
  • Pack lunch when possible — your host family may offer to make bento. Accept. It saves money and is usually delicious.
