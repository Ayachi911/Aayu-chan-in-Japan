I Spent 800 Yen at the Yokohama Strawberry Festival and It Changed Me Permanently

Akarengasoko. Red Brick Warehouse. The place that gave me a strawberry addiction I have no interest in curing.

✎ Ayachi Samyal 🍓 Yokohama, Japan 🍓 6 min read
🍓 800 yen well spent
📍 Yokohama
🏮 Akarengasoko
permanently strawberry-coded

In India, strawberries came small, tart, and slightly suspicious — the kind of fruit you ate because it looked like it should taste like strawberry shortcake but didn't quite follow through on the promise. I liked them okay. They were cute. I had no idea what was coming.

🍓
the cake. the one. ¥600-something of heaven // waterfront, Yokohama ✦

The Red Brick Warehouse and What Lives There

🏮
Yokohama Red Brick Warehouse
横浜赤レンガ倉庫 — Yokohama Akarengasoko

A pair of early 1900s heritage brick warehouses on the Yokohama waterfront, converted into a shopping and events complex. One stop from Sakuragichō on the Minatomiraisen, or a long walk from the bay. Famous for hosting seasonal festivals — strawberry fairs, chocolate fairs, German Christmas markets. Also: the setting for the Yokohama chapters in Bungo Stray Dogs, which means if you're an anime person you will recognize the building immediately and lose your mind a little.

My school was in Yokohama. The eki stop before mine — one single stop — was the station that dropped you at the doorstep of the waterfront, and from there a ten-minute walk to the Red Brick Warehouse. I made this journey several times that year. But the strawberry festival was the one that left a mark.

me, arriving at the festival: "okay I'll just look around, maybe get one small thing, nothing crazy—"

[exits 800 yen later with two strawberry items, having almost genuflected in front of a large strawberry sculpture]

The festival was the kind of thing Japan does with seasonal produce — complete, maximalist, entirely committed. Strawberry everything. Strawberry cakes, strawberry daifuku, strawberry crepes, strawberry parfaits, strawberry merchandise. And everywhere, the actual fruit itself: glossy, enormous, so red it looked almost lit from within.

👑
THE SCULPTURE. THE ONE. standing before the red brick warehouse like it owns the place. because it does. 🍓
There was a large strawberry sculpture at the festival. On a plinth. In front of the entire Red Brick Warehouse. Taller than a person. Glossy. Perfectly red. With little moulded seeds and big green leaves at the base like a crown. Signs around it said DO NOT STEP ON IT, DO NOT SIT ON IT — in both Japanese and English — which tells you everything about how much people wanted to interact with this strawberry. I would have prayed to it. I would have left offerings. I took seventeen photos instead.
approaching the warehouse — you can feel it before you see it
the waterfront side of the warehouse — where I ate my cake on a bench 🌊

It was maybe 1.5 meters tall. Lacquered. So red it looked like someone had decided to paint the concept of strawberry in three dimensions.

— me, still thinking about the sculpture, years later
from above — note the gold leaf 🌟
the height of the cream situation

800 Yen. Two Items. Zero Regrets.

SPENT ¥800 worth it

I spent 800 yen. For context — that is roughly the price of a convenience store meal deal, or two canned coffees, or one mid-tier onigiri and a bottle of green tea. I spent it on two strawberry items from Aki's Sweets Shonan Fujisawa, which had a stall at the festival, and I felt no guilt whatsoever.

Item one was the cake. A mille-feuille situation — flaky pastry layers, clouds of fresh whipped cream, six to seven large strawberries arranged with the commitment of someone who takes their fruit architecture seriously. One of the strawberries had a leaf still attached and was perched on top of a cream swirl like a tiny throne. There was, I believe, a gold leaf accent on one of the strawberries. Gold. Leaf. On a strawberry.

These were not Indian strawberries. These were not the slightly tired ones I'd find at a Delhi market. These were Tochigi or Saga or Fukuoka strawberries — grown for size, grown for sweetness, grown with the full weight of Japanese agricultural perfectionism behind them. They were large. They were bright. They were so sweet that eating one felt like the concept of sweetness agreeing to show up in physical form.

The cream was cold and fresh and not too sweet, which meant it let the strawberries do all the talking. I ate it on a bench near the waterfront. There were boats. There was winter sun. I think I may have made a sound.

THEY WERE BIG SWEET AND SO BRIGHT LOOKING. WITH CREAM THEY WENT MHMHHHMMM.

— me, to my phone notes, immediately after eating
🍓

The Festival, The Place, The Vibe

横浜市営バス — getting there
the sky near the waterfront
chocolate model of the building itself 🍫

The Red Brick Warehouse in winter is a specific kind of beautiful — the old brickwork under cold blue sky, the harbor right there, the mountains somewhere behind the clouds. The festival stalls were set up along the outer courtyard and inside the warehouse floors. Seasonal festivals there always feel slightly too good for how accessible they are. One train stop. That's all it took.

There was a large strawberry sculpture at the event. Decorative, promotional, absolutely unnecessary, completely wonderful. I would have taken a photo with it. I would have talked to it. I respected it as an artistic statement about what strawberries deserve.

pottering cat stall — I browsed but did not stray 🐱
glass plates — gorgeous and did not come home with me

The warehouse had other stalls too — crafts, glass art, the cat accessories shop I definitely browsed for ten minutes without buying anything. But I was there for one reason. I spent my 800 yen with focus and intention, walked to the waterfront bench, and ate my cake in the January cold like a person who had made excellent choices.

🍓

What Happened After: A Full Conversion

I went back to India eventually. And something had changed. The strawberry obsession that started in Yokohama did not leave with my suitcase. It followed me home, took up residence, and began expanding its territory.

The evidence:

  • Nivea strawberry lip balm (daily use)
  • The Body Shop strawberry hand cream
  • Strawberry ceramic cup + saucer
  • Strawberry printed plates
  • Strawberry gifted for Valentine's Day
  • Friend saw a girl fussing over strawberries in Canada. Thought of me immediately.
  • Besties gift me strawberry items unprompted
  • All my friends know. They all know.
Harshita, working at a superstore in Canada: sees small girl creating a fuss over strawberries

→ immediately texts me about it

this is my legacy. this is what I have built.

Harshita works part-time at a superstore in Canada. She saw a little girl making a complete fuss over strawberries and her first thought was of me. That is — I think — the highest compliment I have ever received. A child in Canada having a meltdown about fruit, and my friend across an ocean thinking: ah yes, Ayachi energy.

In India they were small and tart, so I thought they were just cute — like strawberry shortcake the cartoon made them seem. But when I went to Japan and tasted what a strawberry could actually be when it was grown with full agricultural seriousness and eaten with fresh cream in January by a waterfront — something clicked. Something was activated. There is no going back.

I think about the Yokohama strawberry festival the way some people think about a meal they'll never stop chasing — that one lunch in some city that recalibrated their understanding of what food could be. Mine happened to be a mille-feuille on a paper plate at a winter festival, eaten on a harbor bench, for eight hundred yen.

The strawberry found me in Japan. Everything since has been the aftermath.

— Ayachi, still strawberry-coded, still no regrets
