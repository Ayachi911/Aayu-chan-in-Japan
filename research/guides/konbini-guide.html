Convenience Store Guide (From Someone Who Lived On Them)

What to actually buy at 7-Eleven, FamilyMart, and Lawson in Japan — tested daily as a Yokohama exchange student. Not a listicle. A survival document.

7-ELEVEN · FAMILYMART · LAWSON

The first time I walked into a Japanese convenience store — a FamilyMart three minutes from my host family's apartment in Yokohama — I stood in the entrance for a full sixty seconds doing nothing. The smell hit first: something warm and slightly sweet, the ghost of steamed nikuman, fresh coffee from a machine, maybe floor cleaner underneath it all. Then the rows of things. The sheer density of things, all in perfect arrangement, under lights so bright and even it felt like being inside a very clean dream.

I bought a can of green tea that cost ¥110 and didn't know what else to do with myself. By the end of the year I was buying onigiri at 7:30 AM, sipping hot miso soup from a paper cup on the train platform, and genuinely capable of spending twenty minutes just browsing the snack aisle without buying anything. The konbini had become, in the way only Japan can manage, completely mundane and still somehow extraordinary every single time.

This is not a list of "things tourists should try." This is what I actually bought, what I actually ate, and what I genuinely recommend — built from almost a year of daily visits as a 16-year-old who was very hungry and had about ¥1,000 a day to spend.

The konbini is not a corner shop. It is a philosophy. A 24-hour, fluorescent-lit argument that life should be both convenient and delicious.

— a thing I thought on the train home, eating my third onigiri of the week

01 The Three Chains
7-Eleven vs FamilyMart vs Lawson

You will have opinions about this by month two. Every exchange student does. The three main konbini chains each have a personality, and knowing which is which will save you from wandering into a Lawson at 8 AM when you needed a 7-Eleven lunch box.

1
7-Eleven Japan
セブン-イレブン
Best onigiri. Best sandwiches. Dashi-based hot food that is genuinely, shockingly good. The clear overall winner for food quality.
Food First
2
FamilyMart
ファミリーマート
Best fried chicken (FamiChiki is a religious experience). Better snack variety. Nicer seating in many locations. My personal daily driver.
Daily Driver
3
Lawson
ローソン
Best sweets and desserts. Premium Lawson stores carry a different calibre of pastry. Uchi Café range is excellent. Underrated for snacks.
Dessert Win
The honest answer

They are all good. Japan's convenience store quality across the board is so far above anything comparable elsewhere that ranking them is almost beside the point. The real answer is: go to whichever is closest. Then go to the others too.

02 What to Actually Buy
The Honest, Tested List

I kept a rough mental log of my konbini spending — the kind of accounting you do when you're on a student stipend and every ¥100 counts. What follows are the things I went back to repeatedly, not because I was trying to "experience Japanese culture" but because they were simply, genuinely, the best version of what they were.

Onigiri (おにぎり) — the ¥110–¥180 meal

The single most important konbini item. A rice ball wrapped in nori, filled with something — tuna mayo, salmon, mentaiko, pickled plum — and engineered for perfect eating via a three-step plastic wrapper that deserves its own TED Talk. The 7-Eleven onigiri is the benchmark. Specifically: the salmon onigiri at 7-Eleven, eaten cold on a train platform at 7:40 AM, is one of the better breakfasts I have had in my life, and I say this with full seriousness.

The onigiri wall. Every morning. This is where decisions get made.

FamiChiki — ファミチキ

FamilyMart's fried chicken. Available hot, from the counter, at any hour of the day or night. There are no circumstances under which this is a bad idea. I have eaten FamiChiki at 11 PM after a school cultural festival and it tasted exactly like victory. ¥220.

Hot drinks from the machine

Every konbini has a machine that makes fresh coffee, hot chocolate, and several varieties of tea. In winter — and Yokohama winters are mild but damp and grey in a way that gets into your coat — this machine becomes essential. A hot café latte for ¥150 is one of the quiet pleasures of Japanese daily life that nobody thinks to mention.

Miso soup (味噌汁) from the hot foods section

Poured from a machine or available in small cups near the register. It costs about ¥100 and it is actual, real miso soup. This is not a travesty. This is just what Japan is like.

Ate konbini miso soup every morning for a month during exams because it was the only warm thing I had time for before the 8:03 train. Still don't regret it. Japan is doing fine with its konbini food, I promise.

03 The Budget Reality
How Far ¥1,000 Goes

As a Kakehashi exchange student, my daily spending money was limited enough that I had to think about konbini spending carefully. Here is a realistic breakdown of what a full day of konbini meals actually costs — which is relevant if you're on a student stipend and trying to figure out whether you can eat there every day. (You can. Easily.)

Full Day on Konbini Food Yokohama · 2021 prices
Breakfast — onigiri (salmon) + canned green tea ¥270
Lunch — hot bento box (weekday special) ¥480–¥580
Afternoon snack — FamiChiki or egg salad sandwich ¥180–¥230
Evening — onigiri ×2 or instant ramen (cup) ¥200–¥320
TOTAL DAILY SPEND ¥1,130–¥1,400

You can eat three real, satisfying meals and a snack for under ¥1,500. In a country where a sit-down restaurant lunch starts at ¥800 and a family dinner is easily ¥2,000–¥3,000 per person, this is remarkable. The konbini is how Japan makes daily life functional on a student budget.

04 The Snack Aisle
A Brief Philosophy

The konbini snack aisle operates on a principle I came to deeply respect: seasonal rotation. Every major season brings a wave of limited-edition flavours that replace the previous season's limited-edition flavours with no apology and no warning. You learn to buy things when you see them because they may not be there next week.

A few items that I would specifically recommend not missing under any circumstances:

Pocky variations beyond original chocolate. The seasonal flavours — matcha, strawberry, almond crush — are genuinely better than the standard version and cost the same ¥150–¥180. The Giant Pocky format also exists and is an objectively good life choice.

Kinoko no Yama vs Takenoko no Sato. This is Japan's great cultural debate. Mushroom-shaped versus bamboo shoot-shaped chocolate biscuit snacks. I am Team Takenoko and I will not be debating this. Both are ¥200 and both are correct.

Koala no March. Little koala-shaped crackers with chocolate filling, each one printed with a small drawing. I ate these constantly. At ¥120, they are essentially free. There are collector sets with rare designs. I am not ashamed of caring about this.

The one thing worth splurging on

Lawson's Uchi Café premium pudding (プリン). It is about ¥250–¥300, which is not cheap for a konbini item, but it is a small, very cold, very serious pudding in a glass jar and it is substantially better than it has any right to be. Buy it once just to understand.

05 Underrated & Overlooked
The Stuff Nobody Talks About

Konbini guides online tend to cover the same twelve items. Here are the things that don't appear on most tourist lists but that I found myself returning to consistently.

The stationary section

Every konbini sells pens, notebooks, sticky notes, and basic stationery. The quality is surprisingly high — these are Japanese pens, after all — and the prices are reasonable. I replaced my pen at a FamilyMart at least twice rather than going to a proper stationery shop. Sometimes you are already at the konbini. The pen is right there.

Print services

The large multifunction printers in the back of every konbini print documents, scan, copy, and even print photos from your phone. I used this for school assignments, group project materials, and at one point, a printed map of Harajuku because my phone was at 3% battery. ¥10 per page. Works better than most dedicated print shops.

Atm at odd hours

7-Eleven ATMs accept international cards reliably. The fees are low. They work at 2 AM. This is extremely relevant information for exchange students whose host family's bank is not in the same prefecture as where they actually are.

The seasonal hot foods you walk past

Near the register, there is always a heated display. In winter it contains nikuman (steamed pork buns), which are ¥150 and which you should eat immediately upon purchase while standing outside in the cold because that is the only correct way to eat nikuman.

06 Why the Konbini Actually Matters
More Than Just Shopping

Near the end of my exchange year, I spent a lot of time in the konbini around the corner from school for reasons that had nothing to do with food. It was warm. It was bright. On hard days — the kind that are hard in a specific way when you are 16 and far from home and not sure you're doing any of this correctly — the konbini was simply a place you could exist without anyone needing anything from you.

You could buy a ¥110 green tea and stand there for a moment and feel, in some small way, like a person who belonged in the city. Like someone for whom Japan was just where they lived. There is something in that which is not really about the food at all.

But also, the onigiri genuinely is that good. Both things are true.

The konbini is where Japan shows you what it thinks daily life should feel like: efficient, clean, warm, and full of options. It is a reasonable philosophy for a convenience store to have.

— closing thoughts, on the train home from Yokohama one last time
