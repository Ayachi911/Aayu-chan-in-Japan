Exchange Student Diaries · Vol. 03

What My Host
Family Fed Me
in Japan

A year's worth of dinners at a wooden table in Yokohama — birthdays and New Year's Eve, cold nights with hot nabe, biryani made together, and a welcome spread I still think about.

🕖 Dinner every night at 7:30 pm sharp
🏠 Host family in Yokohama, Japan
🍜 Cuisines cooked: Japanese, Chinese, Vietnamese, Spanish, Indian
📅 Full year: Kakehashi 2021

The table was always set by 7:30.

My host family had a rule — unstated but absolute. Bath between 7 and 7:30, then dinner. Everyone at the table together. Ten minutes late and you felt it in the air. There was something deeply grounding about that rhythm — the certainty that every single evening, no matter what kind of day it had been, there would be food. Made carefully. For you.

My host mom cooked from everywhere. Japanese, Chinese, Vietnamese, Korean, Spanish, Indian — not fusion, but proper dishes from each culture, researched and prepared with genuine curiosity and love. She cooked like it was a way of asking questions. And it was.

This is a document of the meals I remember most. Every dish described is real, pulled from the actual photos I took — because I knew, even then, that I was going to miss this table more than I could put into words.

🕖 19:30 — dinner time. always. without question.
01

Day One · First Night in the House

The Welcome Dinner

Grilled Salmon · Chicken Teriyaki · Kimchi Tofu Nabe · Somen · Broccoli

I had just arrived. My suitcase was still in the hallway. I didn't know where the bathroom was or what the morning schedule looked like or how anything in this house worked — and I was sitting at this warm wooden table, and there was already so much food on it.

Let me walk you through it, left to right. A big white plate piled with chicken thighs, dark and glazed with something savoury, alongside florets of bright green broccoli. Next to it, a long oval dish with two thick slabs of grilled salmon — the deep orange kind, skin-on, caramelised at the edges. Then a clay donabe pot of kimchi tofu nabe still simmering, the broth an angry red-orange with big white cubes of silken tofu bobbing gently inside. And to the right, a glass bowl heaped with something pale and shredded — somen, I think, thin wheat noodles, cold and delicate.

Each place had its own chopstick rest. Each chopstick rest had a different little painted motif. The glasses were already filled with pale barley tea. It looked like a restaurant.

the message of that dinner was: you are safe here. you will be fed. nobody said it out loud. nobody had to 🧡
02

A Weeknight · "You've Never Had Ramen?"

The Chashu Ramen She Made
Just for Me

Chashu Pork · Soft-boiled Egg · Narutomaki · Bean Sprouts · Komatsuna · Soy Broth

I mentioned, fairly casually, that I had never had ramen before. Real ramen — not the instant kind that comes in a brick. My host mom looked at me with an expression I can only describe as concerned. A few days later: the bowl.

Look at it. A deep white bowl, wide enough to swim in. The broth is a clear amber-brown shoyu base — soy sauce and dashi and something slow-cooked, deepened by time. Resting across the top is a thick, curling slab of chashu pork belly, the fat rendered translucent and silky, the meat pulling apart at the edges. A narutomaki — that little fish cake with the pink spiral stamped through the centre — floats near the left. A soft-boiled egg sits halved at the front, the yolk that perfect jammy orange that means it was cooked for exactly six minutes and marinated overnight. A small pile of crisp bean sprouts on the right. A few leaves of dark green komatsuna, blanched and soft, tucked in the corner.

I stared at it for a full thirty seconds before picking up the chopsticks. it looked exactly like the ramen in anime. because that's where anime gets it from. BECAUSE IT'S REAL.

I ate every noodle. I ate the egg. I ate every bit of the pork. And then I came to the broth — all that deep, savoury, irreplaceable broth — and I ran out of stomach. I drained it away. I think about it still. I'm sorry.

AAAA THE BROTH. I had to pour it away. the most regrettable decision of the exchange year. I am so sorry. ごめんなさい 😭

Family Activity · Everyone at the Table

The Gyoza Night

There are evenings that feel like what the whole exchange year was supposed to be. You're not a guest anymore, not quite a daughter, but something in between — and everyone is folding dumplings together at the kitchen table and the filling smells incredible and you are, very quietly, the happiest you have been in months.

03

Family Night · Handmade

Two Plates of Gyoza We Folded Together

Pan-fried Pork & Cabbage Gyoza · Two Plates · Different Folding Skill Levels

Host dad and host mom at the counter: prepping the filling — ground pork, finely shredded napa cabbage, garlic, ginger, a little sesame oil, soy. My host sisters and me at the table: a stack of round wrappers, a small bowl of water to seal the edges, and absolutely no technique between us.

The proof is in the photo. The top plate — the beautiful teal one with the terracotta rim — is the parents' work. Each gyoza is perfectly crescent-shaped, neatly pleated along the top in five or six little folds, all leaning together in a clockwise fan like they were stamped by a machine. The bottom plate is ours. Also a fan shape, technically. The pleating is... expressive. Some have three folds. Some have one dramatic fold. One of them has been sealed with what I can only describe as enthusiasm rather than technique.

Both plates went into the pan with a slick of oil, fry until the flat bottom is a deep golden-brown, then a splash of water and a lid slammed on top to steam, then the lid off again for the final crisp. You eat them with a dipping sauce of soy and rice vinegar with a dot of chilli oil. You eat far too many. That is the correct number.

mine were genuinely less pretty. they were also genuinely delicious. skill is irrelevant. love is the ingredient. 餃子!!! 🥟
04

We Cooked It Together · Indian Night

The Biryani I Made
with Her

Chicken Biryani · Basmati Rice · Whole Spices · Caramelised Onion · Dutch Oven

My host mom had cooked from so many cultures already — Vietnamese spring rolls, Korean kimchi nabe, Chinese dumplings, Spanish paella — but Indian food was new. She was curious. She asked me about it. And somewhere in that conversation it became: let's do it together.

She handled the heavy cast iron Dutch oven like it weighed nothing. I explained the spices: whole cardamom, cinnamon stick, bay leaves, cloves, a fistful of cumin seeds crackling in hot oil. She listened carefully and nodded and did exactly what I said, and she also did things with her hands that only someone who has cooked for a long time does — tasted without measuring, adjusted without hesitating.

The biryani in the pot: basmati rice fluffy and distinct, each grain separate, tinted a pale gold from the spiced stock it cooked in. Chunks of chicken thigh scattered through — dark and charred at the edges from the high heat, tender inside. Threads of caramelised onion running all the way through the rice, sweet and deeply brown. The spatula in the corner is mid-scoop, the rice pulling apart in that satisfying way that means it's done right.

she said it was oishii and I almost cried in the kitchen. making your home food for someone who genuinely loves it — that hits differently than I can explain 🫶

It wasn't quite how my family makes it at home. It was also exactly right. Both things are true.

05

9th November · 誕生日 · My Birthday

Vietnamese Spring Rolls, a Table Full of Things,
and Five Baby Chicks Instead of a Cake

Spring Rolls · Multigrain Rice · Simmered Chicken & Eggs · Miso Soup · Cream Soup · Pickles · Assorted Sides

My host mom asked what I wanted for my birthday dinner. I said I didn't mind. She made everything. Let me count what's on this table: a fish-shaped blue ceramic plate with fresh Vietnamese spring rolls stacked in a neat crescent — rice paper, translucent, showing the shrimp and green herbs and carrot and lettuce inside. A bowl of multigrain rice — the kind with little black and red grains mixed into the white, so it comes out speckled and nutty. A large plate of simmered chicken thighs in a rich soy-and-mirin braising sauce, with whole soft-boiled eggs nestled between the pieces, their yolks just set, the whites stained a warm amber from the sauce. Two or three different miso soups in wooden bowls with different daikon and tofu combinations. A cream soup in a smaller bowl, pale and smooth. An empty white plate with a serving of white rice. A dish with picked vegetables. A small bowl of something dark on the side. A bottle of Thai sweet chilli sauce because she remembered I liked it. She remembered.

I counted at least eight separate dishes on this table. for my birthday. she just... made everything. I didn't know what to say so I just kept eating 🎉
Hiyoko Cheesecake Cups · Cat-face Crème Brûlée · Birthday Box

Instead of a traditional birthday cake, there was a white box. The box itself had a little bear face cut into the side panels — the kind of box that is already a gift before you open it. Inside: three fluffy yellow baby chick cheesecakes in individual cups, each with two dark chocolate chip eyes and a small triangular orange beak, their heads perfectly round and dusted pale yellow like they just hatched. Behind them, two cat-face crème brûlée cups — that deep amber bruléed sugar top, cracked and golden-brown, shaped into a cat's face with little ear points at the corners.

I refused to eat them for several minutes because they were too cute. then I ate them immediately. they were so, so good. the cheesecake was light and creamy and the brûlée had that perfect crack. 🐥🐱

That's the thing about Japanese desserts — they make food so beautiful you feel guilty eating it. And then you eat it. Because they also make it taste incredible. The beauty is not a trick. It's just how they do things.

06

A Cold Night · Communal Pot

Shiitake Nabe on a
Winter Evening

Shiitake · Silken Tofu · Udon · Napa Cabbage · Carrot · Dashi Broth · Yakisoba · Kimchi · Pickled Ginger · Raw Fish · Kabocha

On cold days — and Yokohama in winter is genuinely, properly cold — my host mom made nabe. The big aluminium donabe sits in the exact centre of the table, resting on a folded newspaper to protect the wood. Inside: a whole landscape. Thick udon noodles coiled at the bottom of pale dashi broth. Big cubes of silken tofu, white and trembling. Four whole shiitake mushrooms, dark and glossy, their caps open and full of broth. Carrot rounds in orange circles. Pale napa cabbage leaves beginning to go soft. A floret of broccoli in the corner. Two pairs of chopsticks — one red-tipped, one plain bamboo — resting across the top, mid-use.

And then all around it, the supporting cast: a turquoise plate of yakisoba on the left — stir-fried egg noodles with carrot and cabbage, glistening with sauce. A small white flower-shaped plate with bright pink pickled ginger slices and a tiny broccoli floret. On the right side: a small blue-and-white plate with pale raw fish, a dish of something dark and simmered, a vivid red bowl of kimchi. A wooden ladle resting across an empty bowl, ready for serving. A wooden spoon nearby. Multiple chopstick rests. Everything placed with the quiet care that characterised every single dinner at that table.

nabe is the most cosy thing I have ever experienced. one pot. shared from the centre. everyone reaching in. the broth gets richer as the evening goes on. I think about it every winter 🍲
Christmas Eve — paella night 🦐🍷
07

24th December · クリスマスイブ

Paella & Red Wine on
Christmas Eve

Prawn Paella · Fried Fish · French Baguette · Grape Juice (Sangria for the adults) · Mixed Salad

Christmas in Japan is not a holiday in any official sense — schools are still in session on the 24th, nothing closes, life goes on. But collectively, somehow, the whole country agrees to eat something festive and be cheerful about it. My host family leaned into this fully.

Christmas Eve dinner was Spanish. A large dark frying pan dominates the table — paella, loaded with whole prawns, their shells still on and bright coral-red from the heat, heads intact, curled over mounds of yellow saffron rice dotted with green pepper and what looks like mushrooms and vegetables cooked down into the base. Beside it, a white plate of fried fish — battered and golden, crisp on the outside. To the left, torn pieces of a pale baguette. A bowl of mixed salad. And around each plate, a deep red drink in a wine glass — proper glasses, with stems. Sangria for my host parents, grape juice for me, but both red and cold and in the same glasses, equally festive.

grape juice in a wine glass on Christmas Eve in Yokohama while eating Spanish paella. I was living LIFE. 🍷✨
08

Host Grandpa's Retirement · お疲れ様でした

The Sushi Spread for
Grandpa's Retirement

Nigiri Assortment · Sashimi · Maki Rolls · Ikura · Tamagoyaki · Karaage · Green Salad

My host grandpa retired in his seventies and the family threw him a proper dinner. The centrepiece of the table is a large black lacquer sushi tray — the kind you'd see in a good restaurant, wider than a person's arm span, heaped with an assortment that takes a moment to fully process.

Starting from the back: thick slabs of sashimi — pale white squid, deep red maguro tuna. In the middle of the tray: nigiri, row after row — pale pink salmon that catches the light, glossy amber yellowtail, fat shrimp draped over their rice pillows, slices of silky white fish I didn't know the name of, a row of tamagoyaki — that sweet rolled Japanese omelette, perfectly rectangular. At the front: maki rolls, tightly wrapped in nori, some with a dot of orange ikura — salmon roe — gleaming on top. A piece of something bright orange on the corner. The whole tray is arranged with the precision of someone who takes food seriously as an art form.

Around it: a glass bowl of shredded green salad. A plate of karaage chicken, crisp and dark. Everyone's individual plates already bearing pieces they'd chosen. The hands of an elderly man visible in the corner — my host grandpa, at his own retirement dinner.

I LOVE SUSHI SO MUCH and this was the sushi that broke my brain. every sushi I eat now is silently compared to that tray. nothing has matched. おめでとうございます ojii-chan 🎌🍣
09

31st December · 大晦日 · New Year's Eve

Toshikoshi Soba & Tempura
to See in the New Year

Toshikoshi Soba · Tempura Prawns · Vegetable Kakiage · Dipping Broth · Miso Side Soup · Pickled Vegetables

New Year's Eve in Japan means toshikoshi soba — literally "year-crossing noodles." You eat them on the 31st to see out the old year, the long thin noodles symbolising a long life and the cutting of the past year's troubles. It's one of those traditions that is both very simple and quietly profound, and my host family observed it exactly.

The table: two large blue-rimmed bowls of soba — cold, I think, or at least room temperature, the grey-brown noodles piled neatly, distinct and slightly nutty-smelling. A deep savoury dipping broth in smaller bowls on the side — tsuyu, made from dashi and mirin and soy. Then the tempura, and there is a lot of it. One large plate stacked with vegetable kakiage — those mixed vegetable fritters bound together in irregular golden discs — shredded carrot and something green and what looks like burdock, all deep-fried until pale gold and slightly lacy at the edges. A second plate with whole prawn tempura — fat prawns in individual battered coats, tails sticking up, three of them, plus more vegetable pieces behind. Assorted little wooden side bowls with miso soup and pickled things tucked around the edges of the table. The whole spread is orderly, unhurried, perfectly calibrated for a quiet evening at home at the end of December.

the broth you dip the cold soba into — it's not the same broth as the nabe, it's not the same as ramen, it's its own thing entirely and it is SO good. savoury and slightly sweet and deeply comforting at the same time. あけましておめでとう 🎍

What that table taught me

I sat at that wooden table for somewhere close to 365 dinners. I was always there by 7:30. I learned to put my chopsticks on their rest when I wasn't using them. I learned to say itadakimasu before eating and gochisousama deshita when I was done. I learned that in Japan, finishing your rice completely is a compliment. I learned that the soup is not a starter — it's concurrent with everything else.

But the bigger thing I learned is harder to name. My host mom cooked from Spanish tradition and Vietnamese tradition and Indian tradition and Korean tradition and her own, all with the same quality of attention. She wasn't performing worldliness. She was just genuinely curious about food, about cultures, about people — and cooking was how she expressed it.

Food was how she asked: how are you doing? Are you homesick? Do you feel at home yet? She never asked those questions directly. She made biryani with me instead. She put a Thai sweet chilli sauce on the table on my birthday because she remembered I liked it. She made toshikoshi soba on New Year's Eve because that's what you do, and she wanted me to know what that felt like.

I think about that table almost every evening. I set mine a little more carefully now, because of her.

— Ayachi, Yokohama 2021 · ごちそうさまでした ✦
