Tokyo Day Log — The Last One

The Day I Didn't Want To End 最後の日 · 渋谷・原宿・大久保 · ヒナちゃんと

Shibuya Scramble Square. Shibuya Sky from the 46th floor. Harajuku crepes and K-pop stores. A 2D Cafe we never got into. Korean hot pot in Okubo. Cherry blossoms and an Instax. Crying on a metro at 7pm. With Hina-chan, my best friend.

I was laidback about the whole thing all day. We both were. We had accepted it — the return, the goodbye, the end of this particular year. We moved through Tokyo like we had all the time in the world. We did not. But we were very good at pretending until 7pm.

01

Shibuya Scramble Square — Morning

渋谷スクランブルスクエア

We started at Scramble Square because it made sense for a last day — a building that is genuinely trying to contain all of Shibuya inside one structure. The spring "Energies" campaign was everywhere: pink and teal posters on every glass door. 2.24 THU — 3.20 SUN. Scramble Point ×2 this weekend. We were not using the app.

Location 01
The Entrance
"Energies" spring campaign. Pink and teal. The mannequin in the window wore a crochet vest over an orange top. I took a photo of it.
Mandatory Stop
Jimmy Choo Mirror Selfie
Yellow Instax out. Two people in a luxury goods floor mirror. Yellow cardigan left, black blazer right. This is what we came here to do.
Must See
The Shiba Inu Island
Approximately one hundred Shiba Inu plushies in matching green outfits on a glowing oval counter. Japan does not do things by halves.

We also passed the NHK exhibit — streaming app, weekly catch-up, 一週間見逃し配信. Hina-chan stood next to the newscaster poster like she was about to deliver the evening news. I photographed that too. We didn't buy anything in the whole building. We just existed in it.

Unexpected
NHK+ Display
一週間見逃し配信. Posing next to the newscaster like this is a completely normal thing to do. Great content she has no opinion about.
From the Window
City View, Morning
The orange construction crane. The scramble crossing visible as a tiny grid of white stripes. People the size of punctuation marks.
What we actually did in Scramble Square

Took the Jimmy Choo mirror selfie. Stared at the Shiba Inu plushies for longer than was strictly necessary. Posed in front of the NHK display. Watched the city from the windows without paying for the official observation deck yet — that came next. No shopping. Just being in a building that was trying very hard to be all of Tokyo at once.

02

Shibuya Sky — 46F, Open Air

渋谷スカイ · 展望施設

Shibuya Sky is the open-air observation deck on the 46th floor. You get tickets — ours were little illustrated cards with a futuristic tower on a pink gradient sky. The tagline says "Open Your Eyes." Which felt very correct for a last day in a city.

FIG. 01

Shibuya from 46 floors up. Orange construction crane. Scramble crossing as a small grid of white stripes. The Loft sign. The UC card building. The whole city laid out like it genuinely never ends, which it more or less doesn't.

Keepsake
The Tickets
"Open Your Eyes." One each. Kept both. The city is visible below through the glass floor behind them.
The Pose
Hands Up, City Behind
Arms up, phones out, the entire city spread behind us. We took photos instead of just looking. That's what you do.
If you go to Shibuya Sky

Book tickets online — queues can be significant. Go on a clear day. Morning has best visibility and lowest crowds. Golden hour is also extraordinary but crowded. We paid around ¥2,000 each and would pay it again without hesitation.

03

Harajuku & Takeshita Street

原宿 · 竹下通り

Takeshita Street is everything you've been told. It is also genuinely crowded, slightly overwhelming, full of things you do not need and will absolutely consider buying, and it smells like crepes and sugar from the moment you turn the corner off Meiji-dori. We had been here before. That doesn't change anything. You go every time.

FIG. 02

Marion Crepes — Patisserie from Harajuku since 1976. There is always a queue. The queue is worth it. Behind it: classic white stucco and red tile, and behind that, a glass tower that says HULIC. Japan puts its old and new in the same frame constantly and it never stops looking exactly right.

Marion Crepes

Since 1976. The menus are displayed as glossy 3D scale models in the window, which is a very sensible way to show people what they're ordering. The paper wrappers are pink with a little text about how crepes are simple, anytime, anywhere, easy to hold. Both of ours were gone before we made it halfway down the block.

Stop 03
Two Crepes
Strawberry chocola cake & cream (¥610) and caramel cheesecake & cream (¥560). Held up for the photo before eating. Correct behaviour.
Discovery
Suga Day
1993.0309. 윤기야 생일 축하해. 방탄소년단 슈가. A full birthday banner outside a K-pop merch store. The level of organisation fans bring to this in Japan is genuinely impressive.
Also Here
Sugar High!
Pink and teal. Checkered floor. A fake candy shop facade inside a beauty store. She is peering around a shelf of rom&nd lip tints. Normal Harajuku afternoon.

Alice on Wednesday & Gachapon

水曜日のアリス is an Alice in Wonderland boutique with gold lettering on white walls, ornate wrought iron, and small coloured jars on a lit counter. It is exactly what it sounds like. The gachapon machines nearby produced a red capsule containing something small that was inspected with the seriousness it deserved.

Hidden Find
Alice on Wednesday
水曜日のアリス. Gold lettering. Little jars on a lit counter. The kind of shop that makes you feel like you stepped into an illustration.
Classic
Gachapon ガチャポン
Bandai capsule machines at ¥300 each. The red ball opened. Whatever was inside was small and worthy of careful inspection.
04

The 2D Cafe — That We Did Not Enter

絵の中のカフェ · 入れなかった
What Happened · 実際のところ

The 2D Cafe is a black-and-white illustrated cafe in Harajuku. Everything inside looks hand-drawn, flat, like a panel from a manga. They serve tapioca, shaved ice, and fresh fruit juice. The sign outside is a white panel with black ink drawings in an ornate frame. It is charming. We wanted to go.

We saw the queue. We looked at each other. We left.

"Some things are left for next time."

This is the correct attitude for a last day. You can't do everything. The 2D Cafe goes on the theoretical future list — which feels more real now because it has a name, a location, and a specific disappointment attached to it. That's how you make sure you come back.

FIG. 03

The queue. The black-and-white illustrated sign. People with significantly more patience than us. 2D Cafe · 絵の中のカフェ · 年中無休 · AM11:00–PM11:00. Next time.

05

Okubo — Korean Town, Hot Soup

大久保 · 韓国料理

Okubo is Tokyo's Korean neighbourhood — one stop from Shinjuku on the Chuo-Sobu line. It is not a tourist attraction. It is a functioning neighbourhood with Korean restaurants, grocery stores, and cosmetics shops, all operating normally. That's what makes it good.

Stop 05
Omu-ni Restaurant
Menu: samgyeopsal, haemul pajeon, tteokbokki, budae-jjigae, bibimbap. Standing outside pointing at it with confidence. This is research.
The Meal
Budae-jjigae 부대찌개
Army stew. Silver pot. Instant noodles, tofu, tteok, kimchi, a raw egg yolk floating on top. Kimbap on the side. Ate slowly. Talked a lot.

That's the thing about a last meal with someone — you eat slowly because there's no reason to rush and every reason not to. The food was spicy and good. The restaurant had that slight purple-lit ambience. We finished everything on the table.

On Okubo

Not a tourist spot — a neighbourhood. The Korean restaurants here are not performing for visitors, they are just Korean restaurants, which is exactly what makes them good. Budget ¥1,500–2,500 per person for a proper sit-down meal with soup and a side.

06

Cherry Blossoms — The Instax Shot

桜 · カワヅザクラ · インスタックスで

We found cherry blossoms somewhere near Shinjuku — an early-blooming kawazu-zakura, the deep pink variety that comes before the standard Somei Yoshino. The petals were that specific shade of vivid pink that feels too saturated to be real. Early March. Blue sky. Almost warm.

FIG. 04

Hina-chan under a kawazu-zakura. Yellow cardigan. She's holding the Instax up over her face rather than being in the photo. I find this extremely on-brand. The tree is full and enormous and a shade of pink that feels unreasonable for nature. I photographed all of it. It came out right.

The Instax Thing

She carried a yellow Fujifilm Instax Mini all day. It was in the Jimmy Choo mirror selfie. It appeared under the cherry blossoms. She documented the day on film while I documented it on a phone. Both versions of the day now exist, in different aspect ratios, at different exposures. Same day.

07

7PM — The Scramble, Then the Metro

スクランブル交差点 · 地下鉄 · さようなら

By the time we got to the scramble crossing it was golden hour. The Netflix Love is Blind Japan billboard. Yakult. L'Occitane. Everybody crossing in every direction at once. The city was performing itself specifically for us, which is how a city feels when you're about to leave it.

FIG. 05

Shibuya scramble at golden hour. Hundreds of people, all directions at once. "ラブ・イズ・ブラインド JAPAN" on a Netflix billboard. Yakult. Tinder. L'Occitane. The full media wall. Light is gold. I am about to leave all of this.

Time Location What Happened Mood
All day Everywhere Laidback. Completely laidback. Both of us, totally fine about the departure. Accepted it. Moving through the city like it was any other day. Fine ✓
18:30 Scramble Crossed the scramble crossing one last time. Yakult billboard. Netflix. Golden light. Very cinematic. Still managing. Still fine
19:00 Metro She was dropping me off. The train came. Something shifted. She cried. I got sad too. The doors closed. That was it. Not fine
The Metro · 地下鉄 · 19:00

I was laidback about the return. I had accepted it. We had been fine all day — crepes and a sky observation deck and a 2D cafe queue we gave up on and it was all manageable. The whole day was manageable.

And then it was 7pm and she was dropping me off and the train was there and something happened to her face.

She broke down on the metro. I got sad too. It wasn't a scene — it was just two people who had spent a whole year in the same city, sharing a school and a language and a hundred small things, running out of the particular version of time that makes all of that possible.

"We promised to meet later in life."

That is not a vague promise. "Later in life" is a real time period. It will arrive. We will both be in it at some point. The promise is specific. The promise is real. That's the thing about a good goodbye — it doesn't have to be permanent to feel like one.

FIG. 06

Shibuya station, evening. Tracks reflected in glass. A train that has already left or is about to arrive — impossible to tell from here. Green signal light. Night version of a city I spent all morning seeing from above.

I've thought about that day more than most days from that year. Nothing extraordinary happened — no revelations, no dramatic events. Just a normal last day in a city that had been mine and was about to not be mine anymore. Those are the days you remember exactly, in order, for a long time.
