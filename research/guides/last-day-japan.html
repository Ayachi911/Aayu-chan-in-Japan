Tokyo Day Log — The Last One

The Day I Didn't
Want to End. 最後の日 · 渋谷 · 原宿 · 大久保 · ヒナちゃんと

Shibuya Sky from the 46th floor. Marion Crepes in Harajuku. A 2D Cafe we abandoned after seeing the queue. Budae-jjigae in Okubo. Cherry blossoms with a yellow Instax. And crying on a metro at exactly 7pm — which neither of us planned.

I was laidback about the whole thing all day. We both were. We had accepted it — the return, the goodbye, the end of this particular year. We moved through Tokyo like we had all the time in the world. We did not. But we were very convincing until 7pm.

01

Shibuya Scramble Square

渋谷スクランブルスクエア

We started at Scramble Square because it made sense for a last day — a building genuinely trying to contain all of Shibuya inside one structure. The spring "Energies" campaign was everywhere: pink and teal posters on every glass door. The mannequin in the window wore a crochet vest over an orange top. I photographed that before we went inside.

Location 01
The Entrance
"Energies." 2.24–3.20. Point up ×2.
Mandatory
Jimmy Choo Mirror
Instax out. Yellow cardigan left, black blazer right.
Must See
The Shiba Inu Island
~100 plushies in matching green outfits. Japan does not do things by halves.

We also found the NHK exhibit — Hina-chan stood next to the newscaster poster like she was about to deliver the evening news, waving at the camera. We spent longer in this building than intended. Zero purchases. Just existing in a place that was trying very hard to be all of Tokyo at once.

What we actually did here

The Jimmy Choo mirror selfie. Staring at one hundred Shibas. The NHK newscaster pose. Watching the city from the windows before paying to go properly up to the roof. No shopping. Somehow still an hour.

02

Shibuya Sky — Open Your Eyes

渋谷スカイ · 46F

The tickets were little illustrated cards — a futuristic tower on a pink gradient sky, the tagline "Open Your Eyes." Very correct for a last day. The floor up top is warm wood. The view is the entire city in every direction, which turns out to be the correct amount of city to see before you leave one.

FIG. 01 Shibuya from the 46th floor. The construction crane is orange. The scramble crossing is a tiny grid of white stripes. The Loft sign. The UC building. The whole city laid out like it never ends — which, to be fair, it more or less doesn't.

If you go

Book tickets online (~¥2,000). Clear mornings: best visibility, lowest crowds. Golden hour is extraordinary but you'll need patience. Keep the ticket stub — it's a good souvenir and it says "Open Your Eyes" which honestly applies to most days.

03

Harajuku — Takeshita Street

原宿 · 竹下通り

Takeshita Street smells like crepes and sugar the moment you turn off Meiji-dori. We had been here before. That doesn't change anything. You go every time.

Since 1976
Marion Crepes
The original. There is always a queue. The queue is worth it. The building behind is old Harajuku stucco meeting a new glass tower — perfectly Japan.
Held Up First
Two Crepes
Strawberry chocola cake ¥610. Caramel cheesecake ¥560. Pink wrappers. Photo before eating. Correct order of operations.

We also found the K-pop merch store with a full Suga birthday banner out front — 1993.0309. Happy Suga Day. The Sugar High store in pink and teal. Alice on Wednesday with its gold lettering and little coloured jars. Gachapon. A red capsule containing something small that we inspected very seriously.

04

The 2D Cafe — That We Did Not Enter

絵の中のカフェ · 入れなかった
What Happened · 実際のところ

The 2D Cafe is an illustrated cafe where everything looks hand-drawn and flat, like a manga panel. They serve tapioca and shaved ice. The sign outside is black ink on white in an ornate frame. It is genuinely charming. We wanted to go.

We saw the queue. We looked at each other. We left.

"Some things are left for next time."

This is the correct attitude for a last day. The 2D Cafe now has a specific disappointment attached to it — which means it goes on the real list, not the vague one. That's how you make sure you come back.

FIG. 02 The queue. The illustrated sign. People with significantly more patience than us. 絵の中のカフェ · 年中無休 · AM11:00–PM11:00. Next time is a real plan now.
05

Okubo — Korean Town, Hot Soup

大久保 · 韓国料理

Okubo is not a tourist attraction — it is a functioning neighbourhood. Korean restaurants, grocery stores, cosmetics shops, all operating normally. That's exactly what makes it good. One stop from Shinjuku on the Chuo-Sobu line.

Stop 05
Omu-ni Restaurant
Samgyeopsal. Tteokbokki. Budae-jjigae. Bibimbap. Pointing at the menu board. This is called research.
The Meal
부대찌개
Army stew. Silver pot. Raw egg yolk on top. Kimbap on the side. Ate slowly. Talked about everything and nothing.
On last meals

You eat slowly because there is no reason to rush and every reason not to. The food was spicy and good and the restaurant had a purple-lit ambience. We finished everything. That part mattered.

06

Cherry Blossoms & the Instax Shot

桜 · カワヅザクラ

Kawazu-zakura — the early-blooming variety, deep pink, unreasonably vivid for early March. We found them near Shinjuku. She held the yellow Instax up over her face and took a photo of the tree instead of letting herself be photographed. I photographed that instead.

FIG. 03 Yellow cardigan. Yellow Instax obscuring her face. Deep pink blossoms, blue sky. She documented the day on film while I documented it on a phone. Both versions exist now, in different aspect ratios. Same day.
07

7PM — Scramble Crossing, Then Metro

スクランブル交差点 · 地下鉄 · さようなら

By golden hour we were at the scramble crossing. Netflix. Yakult. L'Occitane. Hundreds of people in every direction. The city was performing itself specifically for us, which is how a city feels when you're about to leave it.

FIG. 04 Shibuya scramble at dusk. Hundreds of people. "ラブ・イズ・ブラインド JAPAN" on a Netflix billboard. Yakult. Tinder. The full media wall. Light is gold. I am about to leave all of this.

The Metro · 地下鉄 · 19:00

I was laidback about the return. I had accepted it. We had been fine all day — crepes and a sky observation deck and a cafe queue we gave up on. All manageable.

And then it was 7pm and she was dropping me off and the train was there and something happened to her face.

She broke down on the metro. I got sad too. It wasn't a scene — just two people who had spent a whole year in the same city, sharing a school and a language and a hundred small ordinary things, running out of the particular version of time that makes all that possible.

"We promised to meet later in life."

That is not a vague promise. Later in life is a real time period. It will arrive. We will both be in it. That's the thing about a good goodbye — it doesn't have to be permanent to feel like one.

FIG. 05 Shibuya station, evening. Tracks reflected in glass. A train that has already left or is about to arrive — impossible to tell. Night version of a city I saw from 46 floors up this morning.

I've thought about that day more than most days from that year. Nothing extraordinary happened — no revelations, no dramatic events. Just a normal last day in a city that had been mine and was about to not be mine anymore. Those are the days you remember exactly, in order, for a long time.
